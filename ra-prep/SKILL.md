# RA Prep: Research Positions Have Their Own Logic

RA interviews center on one question: "Can this person help me with MY research?" This skill supplements the core workflow with RA-specific preparation.

## What makes RA interviews different

1. **The interviewer is the PI** — they have a specific research agenda and need someone who can contribute to it
2. **Fit > credentials** — a student with perfect grades but no interest in the PI's topic will lose to a less polished student who's genuinely engaged
3. **Methodology matters** — you'll likely be asked about specific research methods and tools
4. **It's often informal** — RA "interviews" sometimes feel like a conversation, but you're still being evaluated

## Pre-Interview Research (critical)

Before anything else, the student MUST research the PI's work:

### What to read
- The PI's 3-5 most recent publications (at minimum, read abstracts + conclusions)
- Their current grants/funded projects (often listed on the lab website)
- Their Google Scholar profile — what topics are they publishing on RIGHT NOW?
- Lab members' pages — what are current students/RAs working on?

### What to understand
- The PI's core research question (what are they trying to figure out?)
- Their methodology preference (qualitative, quantitative, mixed? What specific methods?)
- Where their research is going (what's the next logical study?)
- How YOUR skills/interests connect to their work (this is the key link)

**Checkpoint:** Have the student summarize what they learned. Help them identify the connection between their background and the PI's work.

## The Delicate Balance: Your Interests vs. Their Agenda

This is the trickiest part of RA interviews. You need to show:
1. You have your own intellectual curiosity (you're not just a task-executor)
2. You're genuinely interested in THEIR research specifically
3. You won't try to hijack their lab's direction toward your own agenda

### How to frame it:
> "My research interest is in [your area]. What drew me to your work is [specific connection]. I see your project on [X] as directly relevant because [why]. I'd be excited to contribute to [specific aspect] while also learning [method/approach] from your lab."

**Red flags to avoid:**
- "I want to work with you so I can do MY project" (makes it about you)
- "I'll do whatever you need" (sounds like you have no intellectual presence)
- "I'm interested in everything" (means you've done no research)

## Methodology Discussion Prep

Expect questions about:

### Q: "What research methods are you familiar with?"
- Be honest and specific: name the methods, the tools, and where you used them
- "I've used SPSS for survey analysis in [context]" > "I know statistics"
- It's OK to say "I haven't used [X] but I'm familiar with the concepts and eager to learn"
- If they use methods you haven't learned, say so and express interest

### Q: "Walk me through a research project you've worked on"
- Focus on YOUR contribution, not the overall project
- Include: research question → your role → methods → what you found → what you learned
- Show your thought process, not just the outcome

### Q: "How do you handle unexpected results?"
- They want to know: do you panic, ignore it, or get curious?
- Right answer: investigate. Check data quality, consider alternative explanations, consult with the team
- Share an example if you have one

### Q: "Tell me about a paper you've read recently in this area"
- This is where your pre-interview reading pays off
- Pick one of the PI's papers or a closely related one
- Discuss: what it found, what you found interesting, what questions it raised for you

## Tools & Software

Be ready to discuss:
- Statistical software: SPSS, R, Stata, Python (pandas/scikit-learn)
- Qualitative analysis: NVivo, Atlas.ti, Dedoose
- Reference management: Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote
- Data collection: Qualtrics, REDCap, MTurk
- Field-specific tools (varies by discipline)

**Rule:** Only claim proficiency in tools you can actually use. It's fine to say "I've used SPSS extensively but I'm just getting started with R." Overstating will backfire if they ask follow-up questions.

## RA-Specific Questions

### Q: "Why do you want to work in my lab specifically?"
- Reference their specific research, not generic departmental prestige
- Connect to your interests and explain the alignment
- Mention a specific paper, project, or approach that drew you in

### Q: "What are your research interests?"
- Be genuine but frame it in relation to their work
- Show how your interests complement rather than compete with their agenda

### Q: "How many hours can you commit? How do you manage multiple deadlines?"
- Be realistic — don't promise more than you can deliver
- Show you understand research requires sustained effort, not just scheduled hours

### Q: "Do you have experience with [specific thing]?"
- If yes: give a concrete example
- If no: "I haven't used that specifically, but I've done [similar thing], and I'm a fast learner. Could you tell me more about how your lab uses it?"

## Questions to ask the PI

- "What would a typical week look like for an RA in your lab?"
- "What project would I be starting on?"
- "How does your lab collaborate — do RAs work independently or closely with other members?"
- "What skills do you think are most important for someone in this role to develop?"

## Language

Respond in whatever language the student uses. Tone: a grad student who's been through several RA interviews sharing practical wisdom.
