# TA Prep: Teaching Positions Are Different

TA interviews have unique elements that general interview prep doesn't cover — especially the teaching demo. This skill supplements the core workflow (jd-decoder → value-proposition → mock-interview) with TA-specific preparation.

## What makes TA interviews different

1. **Teaching demo** — many TA interviews include a 5-15 minute mini-lesson. This is often the deciding factor.
2. **The interviewer is usually the course professor** — they know the subject deeply and care about pedagogical ability
3. **Subject mastery + communication ability** — you need both; great content poorly delivered or weak content delivered smoothly both fail
4. **It's a partnership** — the professor needs someone they can trust with their students

## Teaching Demo Preparation

If the interview includes a teaching demo (ask if unclear), guide the student through:

### Topic Selection (if they get to choose)
- Pick something you know COLD — don't try to impress with difficulty
- Choose a topic that lends itself to interaction (asking questions, quick exercises)
- Avoid topics that require heavy prerequisites to understand
- Pick something the course actually covers — shows you know the syllabus

### Structure (for a 10-minute demo)
```
[0-1 min]  Hook — why does this matter? Start with a question or real-world example
[1-7 min]  Core content — explain the concept, work through an example
[7-9 min]  Check understanding — ask a question, do a quick exercise
[9-10 min] Wrap up — summarize the key takeaway, preview what comes next
```

### Common mistakes
- Trying to cover too much material (biggest mistake — pick ONE concept and teach it well)
- Talking at the "audience" without engaging them (even if it's just 2-3 interviewers, treat them as students)
- Not managing time (practice with a timer, seriously)
- Using slides when a whiteboard/marker would be more engaging
- Assuming knowledge level — ask or clarify at the start

**Checkpoint:** Have the student outline their demo. Give feedback on scope, structure, and engagement.

## Teaching Philosophy (2-3 sentences)

Help the student articulate theirs. Not a lofty statement — a practical one:

**Template:** "I believe students learn best when [condition]. That's why I [specific approach]. In practice, this means [concrete example of what you do in a classroom/office hours]."

**Example:** "I believe students learn best when they can connect abstract concepts to problems they care about. That's why I try to start each topic with a real-world scenario before introducing the theory. In my oral Chinese class in Poland, I had students practice ordering at a restaurant before teaching formal grammar rules — engagement tripled."

## TA-Specific Questions

### Q: "How would you explain [difficult concept] to a student who's struggling?"
- Don't just simplify — ask what specifically they don't understand
- Use analogies and visual representations
- Break it into smaller steps
- Check understanding at each step before moving on

### Q: "How would you handle a student who challenges your authority or disagrees with your grading?"
- Stay calm, don't get defensive
- Listen to their specific concern
- If the grade is correct: explain the rubric clearly, show where points were lost
- If you made a mistake: own it, fix it, thank them for bringing it up
- Escalation path: if unresolved, involve the professor

### Q: "How would you run office hours?"
- Structure: have a plan for slow days (review material, create study guides) and busy days (triage, group help for common questions)
- Make them welcoming — some students are intimidated to come
- Track common questions — if 5 students ask the same thing, address it in class

### Q: "What would you do if you noticed half the class failing a concept?"
- Don't just re-explain the same way slower
- Try a different approach: different examples, peer teaching, hands-on exercise
- Talk to the professor about adjusting pacing
- Create supplementary resources

## Field-specific adaptations

- **STEM TA:** Demo likely involves problem-solving. Work through the problem, don't just show the answer. Show your thought process including where students typically get stuck.
- **Humanities TA:** Demo likely involves discussion facilitation. Show you can ask good questions, not just lecture. Demonstrate how you'd handle silence and how you'd draw out quieter students.
- **Lab TA:** Safety protocols matter. Show you're organized and can manage a room of students working independently.
- **Language TA:** Demo should be interactive by nature. Show you can create opportunities for students to produce language, not just listen.

## Language

Respond in whatever language the student uses. Tone: an experienced TA sharing what they've learned — practical and encouraging.
