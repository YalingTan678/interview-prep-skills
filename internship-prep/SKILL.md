# Internship Prep: Industry Interviews as a Student

Industry internship interviews follow different rules than campus positions. They often have multiple stages, they test potential over experience, and "Why this company?" is the question you absolutely cannot fumble. This skill supplements the core workflow with internship-specific preparation.

## What makes internship interviews different

1. **Multi-stage process** — often: application → online assessment → phone screen → final round
2. **They're hiring for potential, not track record** — they know you're a student with limited experience
3. **"Why this company?" is critical** — they want to know you chose THEM, not that you applied everywhere
4. **Behavioral + technical** — most internship interviews combine both
5. **Culture fit matters a lot** — they're investing in training you, so they want someone who fits the team

## Stage-by-Stage Preparation

### Stage 1: Application & Online Assessment (OA)
- **Resume:** Use `resume-tailor` to align your resume to this specific role
- **OA:** If there's a coding challenge, case study, or aptitude test — practice the format. Ask people who've done it before (Glassdoor, Reddit, 一亩三分地 often have details)
- **Cover letter (if required):** Use `value-proposition` framework — connect your background to their needs in 3 paragraphs max

### Stage 2: Phone / Video Screen (15-30 min)
This is a filter. They want to confirm:
- You can communicate clearly
- You're genuinely interested (not mass-applying)
- Your background roughly matches

**Prepare:**
- 60-second intro (your positioning statement from `value-proposition`)
- "Why this company?" answer (see framework below)
- 1-2 questions that show you researched them

### Stage 3: Final Round (30-60 min, sometimes panel)
This is the real interview. Expect:
- Behavioral questions (STAR method)
- Technical/role-specific questions (varies by field)
- Culture fit assessment
- Your questions for them

## The "Why This Company?" Framework

This question kills unprepared candidates. The answer must be specific — not flattery.

**Bad:** "I admire your company's mission and innovation."
**Bad:** "You're a leading company in the industry."

**Good structure:**
1. **Something specific about their product/work** — reference a recent launch, a feature you've used, a project that interests you
2. **How it connects to your interest/experience** — why this specific company, not just this industry
3. **What you want to learn** — shows humility and growth orientation

**Example:**
> "I've been following [Company]'s work on [specific project/product] — I actually used it for [personal context]. My coursework in [relevant area] gave me a foundation in [relevant skill], and I'd love to apply that in a real product environment. I'm especially interested in how your team approaches [specific aspect], and I think I could contribute to [specific area] while learning [what you want to develop]."

**Checkpoint:** Have the student draft their own. Give feedback on specificity.

## Behavioral Questions (STAR Method)

Internship interviewers love behavioral questions because they reveal how you think and work, even with limited professional experience. Student experiences count:

- Class projects → teamwork, deadline management, problem-solving
- Student organizations → leadership, event planning, initiative
- Part-time jobs → customer service, time management, responsibility
- Research/coursework → analytical thinking, persistence, learning from failure

### The top 5 behavioral questions for internships:

**Q1: "Tell me about a time you worked on a team and there was conflict"**
→ Show you can navigate disagreement constructively. Don't badmouth the other person.

**Q2: "Tell me about a time you failed or made a mistake"**
→ They want self-awareness and learning ability. Pick a real failure, own it, say what you learned.

**Q3: "Tell me about a project you're proud of"**
→ Pick something relevant to the role. Focus on YOUR contribution and the result.

**Q4: "How do you handle competing priorities?"**
→ Show you can triage. Use a specific example from school or work.

**Q5: "Tell me about a time you had to learn something new quickly"**
→ As a student, this is your whole life. Pick an example that shows resourcefulness.

**For each:** Prepare a STAR-structured answer (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Keep each under 2 minutes.

## Technical Interview Basics (by field)

This varies enormously. Brief guidance:

- **CS/Engineering:** LeetCode-style problems, system design basics, "tell me about a project." Prep on platforms like LeetCode, HackerRank. Focus on thinking out loud.
- **Business/Consulting:** Case study interviews. Practice with case books. Structure your answer before diving in.
- **Finance:** Technical questions on accounting, valuation, markets. "Walk me through a DCF." Know the basics cold.
- **Marketing/Communications:** Portfolio review, campaign analysis, trend awareness. Bring examples of your work.
- **Research/Data:** Statistics fundamentals, SQL/Python, experimental design. "How would you measure X?"

For depth, point students to field-specific resources — this skill covers the common patterns, not field-specific technical prep.

## The "Potential Over Experience" Framing

As a student, you'll often feel underqualified. The reframe:

> Companies hire interns for **potential**, not polished expertise. They're looking for:
> - **Learning speed** — can you pick things up quickly?
> - **Initiative** — do you wait to be told or figure things out?
> - **Curiosity** — do you ask good questions?
> - **Coachability** — can you take feedback and improve?
>
> Your coursework, projects, and student activities ARE evidence of these qualities. Frame them that way.

**Instead of:** "I only have class project experience"
**Say:** "In my [course] project, I [what you did]. I taught myself [tool/method] in two weeks to get it done, and the result was [outcome]."

## Questions to Ask the Interviewer

- "What would a typical week look like for an intern on this team?"
- "What project would I be working on this summer?"
- "What do your most successful interns have in common?"
- "How does the team give feedback to interns?"
- "Is there a path from internship to full-time?"

## Language

Respond in whatever language the student uses. Tone: a friend who interned there last summer — practical, honest, and encouraging.
