# GA Prep: Student Services Positions

GA interviews in student services are their own world — they care about mission alignment, DEI competency, and how you handle emotional situations. This skill supplements the core workflow with GA-specific preparation.

## What makes student services GA interviews different

1. **Mission alignment is everything** — they want to know you genuinely care about the office's purpose, not just the funding
2. **Scenario-based questions dominate** — expect "A student comes in and..." questions
3. **DEI is not optional** — inclusive service is a core competency, not a bonus
4. **The interviewer is usually a professional staff member** with a counseling, student affairs, or higher ed background
5. **Soft skills ARE the job** — empathy, communication, cultural humility, event planning

## Mission Research (do this first)

Before anything else, understand the office's mission:

- Read their mission statement on the website. Read it twice.
- Look at their programs, events, and services — these tell you what they actually DO, not just what they say
- Check annual reports if available — what are they measuring? What are they proud of?
- Understand where they sit in the university structure (Division of Student Affairs? Academic Affairs? Enrollment Management?)

Then articulate YOUR connection to that mission. Not "I want to help students" — specifically WHY this office and this kind of work.

**Checkpoint:** Have the student explain in 2-3 sentences why they want to work at this specific office. If it sounds generic, push them to be more specific.

## The Scenario-Based Question Framework

These questions test your instincts. There's a pattern to answering well:

### The 3-step framework: Acknowledge → Understand → Act

1. **Acknowledge the emotion first** — before solving anything, show you see the person
   - "I can see this is really frustrating for you"
   - "That sounds like a really stressful situation"
2. **Understand the root cause** — ask questions before jumping to solutions
   - "Can you tell me more about what happened?"
   - "What's the most important thing for you right now?"
3. **Move to action together** — not "here's what you should do" but "let's figure this out"
   - "Here's what I think we can try — what do you think?"
   - "Let me connect you with [resource] who specializes in this"

### Practice scenarios (adapt to the specific office):

**"A student comes in upset about a rejection"**
→ Acknowledge → Understand what specifically hurts → Reframe (rejection is data, not verdict) → Concrete next steps

**"A student is angry about a service they received"**
→ Acknowledge without being defensive → Listen to the specific complaint → Apologize if appropriate → Fix or escalate

**"A student doesn't know what they want to do with their life"**
→ Normalize the feeling → Explore interests through questions (not advice) → Suggest small exploratory steps → Follow-up plan

**"Two students in a program disagree and escalate to you"**
→ Hear both sides separately → Find common ground → Facilitate conversation → Document if needed

## DEI Competency

"I value diversity" is not an answer. Prepare:

1. **A specific example** of working across cultural difference — what happened, what you learned, what you'd do differently
2. **Awareness of structural barriers** — not just cultural differences but systemic obstacles (visa restrictions, first-gen challenges, disability access, financial constraints)
3. **Cultural humility** — the recognition that you DON'T know everything about other people's experiences, and that's OK as long as you listen and learn
4. **Proactive inclusion** — not just serving diverse students who show up, but reaching out to communities that underuse services

## Career Development Theories (for career center GAs)

If the GA is at a career center, the interviewer likely has a counseling background and may value theoretical grounding:

- **Holland's RIASEC** — 6 personality types mapped to work environments (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional). Useful for helping students explore interests.
- **Super's Life-Span Theory** — career development as a lifelong process with stages. Helps normalize "not knowing" for students.
- **Krumboltz's Happenstance Learning Theory** — unplanned events shape careers. Helps students see that exploration and serendipity are valid.
- **Social Cognitive Career Theory (SCCT)** — self-efficacy and outcome expectations shape career choices. Useful for understanding why some students feel stuck.

Don't lecture about these — mention them naturally. "When a student is unsure about their direction, I might use an interest assessment based on Holland's model as a starting point, but I always follow the student's lead rather than letting a framework dictate the conversation."

## GA-Specific Questions

### Q: "Why this office specifically?"
- Reference their mission, a specific program, or something you noticed during research
- Connect to your personal experience or values

### Q: "How would you balance this GA with your coursework?"
- Be concrete: how many hours of coursework, what's your plan
- Show you've done GAs or similar commitments before
- Mention advisor support if relevant

### Q: "How would you handle a situation outside your expertise?"
- Know when to refer — to other offices, counseling center, campus police
- It's a strength to recognize your limits, not a weakness
- "I would listen, make sure the student feels heard, and then connect them with [specific resource] who can better help"

### Q: "What programming or events would you bring to this office?"
- Propose something specific based on your research of what they already do and what might be missing
- Show you understand their audience and constraints (budget, time, space)

## Language

Respond in whatever language the student uses. Tone: a GA who's been through it and wants to help you succeed.
